Module 0 · 20 min

The one question everything follows from

A physical object has infinitely many points. A computer has finite memory. So the foundational question of all 3D modeling is:

How do you describe a continuous 3D shape with a finite amount of data — such that the operations you care about are fast, exact enough, and don't break?

Every representation in this course is a different answer, and every answer is a trade. The three axes of the trade:

  • Exactness. Is a cylinder actually a cylinder (radius stored as a number, surface evaluated analytically), or an approximation (many flat facets)?
  • Robustness. Do operations (especially booleans: union, subtract, intersect) always succeed, or can they fail on edge cases?
  • Queryability. Can you ask for "that edge," "this face," "the volume," "a section cut at z=1.2m"? Some representations have named parts (topology); others are undifferentiated soup.

Spoiler for the whole day: no representation wins all three. Exact representations are fragile; robust ones are approximate; queryable ones are heavy. That impossibility is why multiple representation families coexist, why every serious tool picks one and optimizes around it, and why translating between tools is so painful (the subject of Course 3).

One more first principle: distinguish the shape from the recipe that made it. "A box minus a cylinder" is a recipe; the resulting solid with its 10 faces is a shape. Systems that store recipes can regenerate when you change a parameter; systems that store only shapes cannot. Hold this distinction — the entire second half of the day (and all of Course 3) rests on it.

Module 1 · 60 min

Sixty years of history in one arc

The history is worth an hour because nearly every "why is it like this?!" you'll ever ask has a historical answer. Click each era.

1963 — Sketchpad: constraints came first

Ivan Sutherland's Sketchpad (MIT PhD, light pen on a vector display) is the big-bang artifact. Astonishingly, it already had constraint solving — you drew sloppy lines and told the system "these are perpendicular, these are equal length," and it solved for the geometry. The industry then took ~25 years to ship that idea commercially. Lesson one of CAD history: the ideas are old; the engineering to make them robust is what takes decades.

1960s–70s — Surfaces from car bodies: Bézier, de Casteljau, Coons, NURBS

Freeform surface math came from the auto industry: Paul de Casteljau at Citroën (1959) and Pierre Bézier at Renault (UNISURF, late 60s) invented control-point curves so designers could specify smooth car panels numerically; Steven Coons (MIT) gave the patch framework. Ken Versprille's 1975 PhD introduced NURBS (Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines), which unify freeform curves AND exact conics (circles, ellipses) in one formula — that unification is why NURBS became the standard exchange currency of surface geometry.

Early 1970s — The two rival answers to "what is a solid?"

Two schools formed, and both of their answers are still under everything today:

  • B-rep (boundary representation) — Ian Braid's BUILD system at Cambridge: describe a solid by its skin — faces, edges, vertices, with connectivity. The Cambridge group's commercial lineage runs BUILD → Romulus → Parasolid (1988) and ACIS (1989, named for founders Alan Grayer, Charles Lang, Ian Braid). Two research colleagues' kernels ended up powering most of world CAD.
  • CSG (constructive solid geometry) — Requicha & Voelcker at Rochester (PADL): describe a solid as a formula — primitives combined by union/intersect/ subtract. Compact, always mathematically valid, but you must evaluate it to get faces you can point at.

Notice: CSG stores a recipe, B-rep stores a shape. The tension of Module 0, already institutionalized by 1975.

1978–87 — Meshes, subdivision, STL

Entertainment and graphics went polygonal: Ed Catmull & Jim Clark's subdivision surfaces (1978) let a coarse polygon cage define a smooth limit surface (Pixar's workhorse). Marching cubes (Lorensen & Cline 1987) converted volume/implicit data to meshes. And 3D Systems' STL format (1987) — raw triangle soup, no topology, no units — became 3D printing's lingua franca, a lowest common denominator we still live with.

1987 — Pro/ENGINEER: the parametric revolution

Samuel Geisberg's PTC shipped Pro/ENGINEER: models as feature histories — sketch, extrude, hole, fillet, pattern — replayed in order, driven by dimensions, with constraint-solved sketches (Sketchpad's idea, 24 years later). Change a dimension, regenerate, everything downstream updates. It ate the MCAD market; SolidWorks (1995) brought the same paradigm to Windows on the Parasolid kernel. It also created a new disease: the persistent naming problem (Module 3).

2000 — Revit: parametrics become behavioral (BIM)

Two PTC alumni (Leonid Raiz, Irwin Jungreis) asked: what does parametric mean for buildings? Their answer — Revit (acquired by Autodesk 2002) — was not a feature tree but a network of behavioral objects: walls that auto-join, windows hosted by walls, a change-propagation engine keeping plans/sections/schedules consistent. The IFC exchange standard (buildingSMART, from the mid-90s) grew alongside. The behavior lives in compiled application code, not in any file — which is precisely why BIM interop is hard (Course 3).

2007–now — Dataflow, direct modeling, implicits, code, AI
  • Grasshopper (2007, on Rhino): parametric modeling as a visual dataflow graph — architects' favorite; no history tree, just a program you can see.
  • Direct modeling (SpaceClaim 2007, Siemens Synchronous Tech): push/pull faces on dumb B-rep, inferring intent — a reaction against brittle history trees.
  • Implicits return (nTop, 2015+): fields instead of surfaces, because lattices and topology-optimized parts break B-rep booleans (Module 2).
  • Code-CAD (OpenSCAD 2010, CadQuery, build123d, Zoo/KCL): the recipe as literal source code — git-diffable, and lately, the representation LLMs prefer to write.
  • AI lifting (2018→): neural nets and program synthesis that recover recipes from dumb geometry — decompilers for shapes.

Module 2 · 2 h — the heart of the course

The representation families — and NURBS, the thread through them

2.1 Meshes — the shape as facets

Math: a list of vertices (points) and faces (usually triangles) connecting them. Optionally with connectivity structure (half-edge data structures) so you can walk from a face to its neighbors.

Strengths: universal (GPUs eat triangles), simple, robust to render, and — with modern exact arithmetic — booleans on meshes can be made bulletproof (this is what the Manifold library, OpenSCAD's engine, does). Weaknesses: approximate (curvature is gone; a "cylinder" is N flat rectangles), heavy for smooth shapes (quality costs polygons), and semantically flat — there is no "the top face," just triangles 4,017–4,562. Watch for the manifold/watertight distinction: triangle soup (STL) may have gaps, duplicate vertices, self-intersections; solid modeling on meshes requires a closed, consistently-oriented, non-self-intersecting surface — a manifold mesh.

Where: games/film (OBJ, glTF), 3D printing (STL, 3MF), scanning, simulation input, viewers (Navisworks tessellates everything for review speed).

2.2 B-rep — the shape as an exact skin with named parts

Math: two coupled layers. Topology: a graph of vertices → edges → loops → faces → shells → solids ("what touches what"). Geometry: each face carries an exact surface (plane, cylinder, sphere, torus, NURBS), each edge an exact curve, each vertex a point ("where things are"). Faces are trimmed: the surface is infinite; the loops of edges bound the used region.

Strengths: exact (πr²h volumes to machine precision — you saw this in the repo's demo where OCCT's colonnade matched closed form to the decimal); has named parts, which is what makes fillets ("round this edge"), drawings, dimensioning, machining, and quantity takeoff possible. Weaknesses: heavy; and booleans require surface–surface intersection — numerically hairy code full of heuristics for tangencies and near-coincidence. B-rep booleans can fail. This fragility is the deepest engineering fact about CAD kernels.

Where: every serious MCAD/AEC authoring tool: Parasolid (NX, SolidWorks, Vectorworks…), ACIS/ShapeManager (AutoCAD), CGM (CATIA), OCCT (FreeCAD — open source), plus Revit's proprietary kernel. Files: STEP, and each kernel's native format.

2.3 NURBS — the freeform math, and surface modeling as a paradigm

Math: a NURBS curve/surface is a piecewise-polynomial blend of control points: each point pulls the shape toward itself with an influence defined by a degree (how smooth), a knot vector (where influence regions begin/end), and a per-point weight. Move a control point, the surface follows — sculpting with a cage instead of touching the surface.

The "R" is the genius part. NURBS = Non-Uniform Rational B-Spline; the weights make it rational, and rational curves can represent conics exactly — a circle, an ellipse, a cylinder — which plain polynomial curves (Bézier/B-spline) mathematically cannot. One formula therefore covers both sculpted freeform panels and exact machine geometry. That unification (Versprille's 1975 thesis, Module 1) is why NURBS became the exchange currency of surface geometry: it's the freeform surface type inside IGES, STEP, and nearly every B-rep kernel.

Continuity is the craft: surfaces meeting with matching position (G0), tangency (G1), or curvature (G2). Automotive "Class-A" surfacing is the discipline of G2+ continuity — reflections on a car body instantly reveal curvature breaks. This is why Gehry's office needed CATIA-grade surfacing (Course 1, Module 1) for fabrication.

NURBS plays two distinct roles — keep them separate in your head:

  • As geometry inside B-rep (2.2): the freeform faces of a solid are trimmed NURBS patches; the kernel guarantees the solid's topology.
  • As a standalone paradigm — surface modeling (Rhino, Alias): you build, trim, and join skins directly; a "solid" is merely a closed skin sealed within the document tolerance. Total sculptural freedom — but watertightness is your job, and downstream solid operations inherit your tolerance sins.

Where: Rhino/Grasshopper (openNURBS), automotive Class-A (Alias, CATIA/ICEM), ship hulls, freeform façades — anywhere surface quality is the product.

2.4 CSG & procedural — the shape as a recipe

Math: a tree (or program). Leaves are primitives (box, cylinder, extrusion of a profile); internal nodes are operations (union, difference, transform, pattern). The shape is defined as "whatever this evaluates to."

Strengths: tiny, perfectly editable (change a leaf parameter, re-evaluate), inherently valid, diffable — this is the "recipe" side of Module 0's distinction, and it's exactly what the recipes/studio_wall.ir file in this repo is. Weaknesses: you can't point at a face (there are no faces until evaluation); the result depends on the evaluator — the same recipe evaluated by two engines gives slightly different geometry (Course 3's central problem); and pure-primitive CSG can't express arbitrary freeform shapes.

Where: OpenSCAD (recipe as code, evaluated by the Manifold mesh engine), IFC's extruded/boolean solids, Tekla's profile+cut parts, every feature-history system secretly (a history tree IS a richer CSG).

2.5 Implicits / SDFs — the shape as a function

Math: a scalar field f(x,y,z); the solid is everywhere f ≤ 0. A signed distance field (SDF) is the special case where |f| is the distance to the surface. A sphere is f = √(x²+y²+z²) − r.

Strengths: booleans become pointwise min/max — they cannot fail, on any input, ever. Offsets ("grow by 3mm") are just f − 3. Perfect for lattices, blends, topology-optimization output — geometry with millions of features where B-rep would explode. Weaknesses: no topology at all — no faces, no edges, nothing to dimension or machine until you reconstruct a surface (marching cubes → mesh); asking "where exactly is the boundary" costs sampling.

Where: nTop, shader/procedural art (Inigo Quilez's SDF ray-marching), medical volumes — and the geomir/backends/sampler.py in this repo, which classifies points against membership functions: a working, 100-line implicit kernel.

Module 3 · 90 min

The parametric layer

Module 2 was about the evaluated shape. This module is about the recipe layer on top — where design intent lives. There are five paradigms; every tool you'll ever meet is one of them (or a hybrid).

3.1 Direct modeling — no recipe

Push and pull the evaluated geometry itself (SketchUp, SpaceClaim, AutoCAD solids). Simple mental model, nothing to regenerate, nothing to break — and no intent retained. "The hole is gone" — which hole? There's no hole; there's an absence of material.

3.2 Feature history — the replayed program

The MCAD standard (Pro/E → SolidWorks, CATIA, NX, Onshape, FreeCAD): an ordered list of operations, each referencing results of earlier ones. Sketches inside features are solved by a geometric constraint solver — and here is a wild industry fact: nearly every competing CAD product licenses the same solver, Siemens' D-Cubed DCM. Rivals share an organ.

Feature history's chronic disease is the persistent naming problem. Work through this slowly — it matters more than anything else in this module:

The persistent naming problem, step by step
  1. Feature 1: extrude a rectangular sketch → a box. The kernel numbers its faces 1–6, edges 1–12.
  2. Feature 2: fillet edge 7 (the reference is stored as "edge 7 of feature 1's result").
  3. You edit the sketch: add a notch to the rectangle. The extrusion now has 8 faces and 18 edges — the kernel renumbers everything.
  4. Feature 2 replays: "fillet edge 7." Which edge is 7 now? Maybe the wrong one. Maybe none. Your model breaks, or worse, silently fillets the wrong edge.

Split and merge make it fundamentally hard: if my referenced edge got cut in two, which half does the name follow? Research has chewed on this since the 90s (Kripac 1997; Marcheix & Pierra's 2002 survey). FreeCAD shipped its fix only in v1.0 (Nov 2024). Onshape's FeatureScript has the most PL-flavored solution: references are stored as queries ("the edge created by feature X from sketch segment Y"), resolved fresh against current topology each regeneration — late binding instead of hard-coded numbers.

3.3 Dataflow graphs — the visible program

Grasshopper (Rhino), Dynamo (Revit), GenerativeComponents (Bentley): nodes are operations, wires are data, the whole recipe is a diagram. It's functional programming with a GUI — no hidden regeneration order, trivially parallel to reason about. Weakness: graphs of real projects grow into thousand-node spaghetti with no abstraction mechanism beyond "cluster."

3.4 Behavioral objects — the recipe as rules (BIM)

Revit families, ArchiCAD GDL objects: the object carries behavior — a wall knows how to join other walls; a window knows how to cut and attach to its host wall; changing a level height propagates to everything on that level. The "program" is a network of domain-specific rules executed by a change-propagation engine. Powerful for buildings; catastrophic for interoperability, because the rules are compiled vendor code — no file format carries them.

3.5 Code-CAD — the recipe as literal source

OpenSCAD, CadQuery/build123d (Python on OCCT), Zoo's KCL: the model IS a program in a text file. Versionable, diffable, reviewable, generatable — and notably the representation of choice for LLM-driven CAD. The repo you're sitting in is a code-CAD artifact: recipes/studio_wall.ir is a recipe in a deliberately minimal language, and out/model.scad is the same recipe lowered to OpenSCAD's language with parameters kept live.

Module 4 · 60 min

Kernels: the industry's hidden engines

A geometry kernel is the library implementing Module 2 for real: the B-rep data structures, the surface intersectors, the boolean algorithms, tessellation, mass properties. Kernels are decades-old, million-line codebases; almost nobody writes a new one. Who runs on what:

Note the irony rows: Dassault (SolidWorks) pays arch-rival Siemens for Parasolid; nearly everyone licenses Siemens' D-Cubed constraint solver. Sharing happens when economics permit — kernels are just usually the moat, not the shared part.

Tolerances: the dirty secret

Floating point can't represent "these two surfaces touch" exactly, so every kernel defines coincidence-within-ε. But they disagree on ε and on where it lives: Parasolid's session precision is ~1e-8 (with per-entity local tolerances — think of edges as thin tubes and vertices as tiny spheres); classic ACIS uses ~1e-6; OCCT attaches tolerances to each shape. Consequences:

  • A solid watertight at 1e-6 has real gaps at 1e-8. The same model can be valid in one kernel and broken in another — with no one at fault.
  • Kernels have private procedural surface types (rolling-ball blends, lazily evaluated intersection curves) that must be approximated on export, shifting geometry by tiny amounts that then violate someone's ε.
  • Hence an entire commercial industry — CADfix, Elysium, HOOPS Exchange — whose product is "healing": re-stitching, re-trimming, and re-tolerancing geometry so the target kernel will accept it. Its 30-year persistence is proof that even dumb-geometry exchange is unsolved.

Module 5 · 45 min

Open challenges — where the field actually is

  1. Boolean robustness. Still not solved for B-rep after 50 years; heuristics near tangencies. Newest attack: exact-arithmetic mesh CSG (TOG 2025) — robustness by construction, at the mesh level.
  2. Persistent naming. Solved-ish within single systems (queries, FreeCAD 1.0); utterly unsolved across systems — the blocker for exchanging editable models.
  3. Interoperability. The recipe/behavior layer doesn't survive any standard exchange (IFC, STEP); evaluated geometry barely survives (tolerances). This is Course 3 entirely.
  4. Scale. Federated building models and 100k-part assemblies: instancing, streaming, level-of-detail — why coordination tools tessellate everything.
  5. The AI wave. Text/image/scan → recipe (DeepCAD, CAD-Recode, Fusion360 Gallery datasets); LLMs writing code-CAD; and lifting dumb geometry back to editable intent — decompilers for shapes. The most active frontier as of 2026, and it pressures the whole industry toward code-like, liftable representations.
Connection to this repo: the demo you can run (python demo.py) is a working miniature of challenge #3: one recipe evaluated by two kernels with different math (OCCT B-rep vs Manifold mesh), with a tolerance contract catching their disagreement, per-element fallback, and source-level lifting. Course 3 turns it into labs.
Module 6 · 30 min

Capstone check

You're onboarded when you can answer these cold (each expands):

1. Why can't I get "the radius" out of an STL file?

STL is evaluated, approximated geometry — triangle soup. The analytic cylinder was destroyed at tessellation; radius is now a curve-fitting estimate, not a stored fact.

2. Why does the same boolean succeed in kernel A and fail in kernel B?

Different tolerance regimes (what counts as "touching") and different heuristic surface-intersection code. Both kernels are self-consistent; the disagreement is structural.

3. Why did fillet fail after I edited an early sketch?

Persistent naming: the fillet referenced a topological entity by identity; regeneration renumbered topology; the reference dangled or bound wrongly. Fix pattern: late-bound semantic queries.

4. Why do lattice/generative tools use implicits?

Field booleans are pointwise min/max — infallible and O(1) per query — where B-rep booleans on a million-strut lattice are combinatorially explosive and failure-prone. The price: no faces/edges until reconstruction.

5. What's the difference between a shape and a recipe, and which do standard exchange formats carry?

Recipe = the parametric program (editable intent); shape = its evaluated result. Exchange formats overwhelmingly carry shapes (plus shallow recipe fragments in IFC), which is why imported models arrive frozen. The consequences and the fix-shaped-hole are Course 3.
